The Importance of Sex Education

Many parents focus on warning children about strangers. But the data tells a different story. RAINN (the Rape, Abuse & Incest National Network) reports that the vast majority of child sexual abuse is perpetrated by someone the child trusts, be it family members, friends of the family, or even a teacher. They report that '93% of juvenile victims knew the perpetrator, 59% were acquaintances, 34% were family members, and only 7% were strangers.' Teaching kids that abuse only comes from strangers can make them more vulnerable to the people they know. Kids need to learn what boundaries, consent, and bodily anatomy are and the fact that they're concepts that apply to everyone, not just selective people. They need to learn that absolutely no one is entitled to touch them in ways that make them uncomfortable.

Parents Are Not Parenting Anymore

There are a ton of examples of such parenting, such as a parent scrolling on their phone while their kid is telling a story, but the moment of connection slips by as the parent remains disinterested. The child learns that their words don't matter, and so they keep silent. Another child remains home due to being sick, but no one is checking up on them at all. Illness becomes just another lonely day. An older brother puts his younger sister to sleep after feeding her and helping out with her homework. And so, neglectful parenting creates parentification of children. The damage from neglectful parenting isn't always visible on the surface, but it runs bone-deep. Research consistently shows that children raised with this parenting style tend to score low across multiple life domains. A 2019 study found that children raised by neglectful parents tend to struggle in school, experience more depression, have worse social relationships, have difficulty controlling their emotions, and experience more anxiety.
